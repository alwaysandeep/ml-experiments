# --- Motivation & Predecessors ---
What problem do RNNs have with long sequences?	Two problems: (1) <b>Sequential processing</b> — can't process word 5 until words 1-4 are done, so GPUs sit idle. (2) <b>Forgetful</b> — the hidden state is a fixed-size vector, so by word 200, information from word 3 is mostly overwritten. It's like a game of telephone through a fixed-size bottleneck.	transformer::motivation transformer::rnn
What is the hidden state in an RNN?	A fixed-size vector (e.g., 512 numbers) that serves as the model's "mental summary" of everything it has seen so far. It is initialized to zeros and updated at each time step: the RNN takes the current word + previous hidden state → produces a new hidden state. The same weights are applied at every step.	transformer::motivation transformer::rnn
What are the three gates in an LSTM and what does each do?	<b>Forget gate:</b> "How much of my old long-term memory (cell state) should I erase?" (0=erase all, 1=keep all)<br><b>Input gate:</b> "How much of this new information should I write into my long-term memory?"<br><b>Output gate:</b> "How much of my long-term memory should I expose as my current hidden state?"<br><br>Each gate outputs values between 0 and 1. The LSTM passes two things forward: hidden state h (working memory) and cell state c (long-term notebook).	transformer::motivation transformer::lstm
Why are RNNs slow to train compared to Transformers?	RNNs process tokens <b>sequentially</b> — you must finish step t before computing step t+1. This means thousands of GPU cores sit idle. Transformers process all positions <b>in parallel</b> because attention computes all word-pair relationships simultaneously via matrix multiplication.	transformer::motivation transformer::rnn transformer::training
How does the LSTM improve on the basic RNN's memory problem?	The LSTM adds a <b>cell state</b> (long-term notebook) alongside the hidden state, with three learned gates that selectively remember/forget information. This lets important information survive longer. But it's still sequential, still a fixed-size bottleneck, and still struggles past a few hundred tokens — just <i>less</i> forgetful, not un-forgetful.	transformer::motivation transformer::lstm
# --- Core Attention Mechanism ---
What are Q, K, V in the attention mechanism and where do they come from?	All three are derived from the same word embedding x via learned weight matrices:<br><b>Q = x · W_Q</b> (Query: "What am I looking for?")<br><b>K = x · W_K</b> (Key: "What do I contain/advertise?")<br><b>V = x · W_V</b> (Value: "What information do I actually carry?")<br><br>W_Q, W_K, W_V are learned during training via backprop, just like any other weights.	transformer::attention transformer::qkv
Explain the networking event analogy for Q, K, V.	A person at a networking event has three aspects:<br><b>Query:</b> "I need a backend engineer" (what they're looking for)<br><b>Key:</b> "I know databases" (their badge — how they describe themselves)<br><b>Value:</b> Their full knowledge and experience (what they actually contribute once found)<br><br>Separating these lets the model distinguish "what makes me findable" from "what I contribute once found."	transformer::attention transformer::qkv
Walk through the three steps of computing attention for one word.	<b>Step 1 — Scores:</b> Compute dot product of my Query with every word's Key. High dot product = high relevance.<br><b>Step 2 — Weights:</b> Apply softmax to scores → probabilities summing to 1.<br><b>Step 3 — Output:</b> Weighted sum of all Value vectors using those weights.<br><br>Example for "sat": scores might give 40% weight to "cat", 28% to "mat". Output = 0.40·V_cat + 0.28·V_mat + ... A context-aware representation.	transformer::attention
Why divide attention scores by √d_k (scaled dot-product attention)?	Large d_k causes dot products to grow large → softmax produces extreme distributions like [0.998, 0.001, 0.001] → gradients vanish for low-weight words → model stops learning.<br><br>Dividing by √d_k keeps scores in a moderate range so softmax produces softer, more useful distributions where all words get meaningful gradients.	transformer::attention transformer::scaling
Show with a toy example why scaling by √d_k matters.	With d_k=64, raw scores might be [64, 60, 58]:<br>softmax → [0.982, 0.013, 0.005] — almost all weight on one word.<br><br>After dividing by √64 = 8: scaled = [8.0, 7.5, 7.25]:<br>softmax → [0.52, 0.33, 0.15] — all words get meaningful weight and gradients.	transformer::attention transformer::scaling
What does softmax do to attention scores?	Converts raw scores (any real numbers) into a probability distribution that sums to 1. Larger scores get exponentially more weight. This determines how much each word's Value contributes to the output. It's extremely sensitive to input magnitude — a difference of 4 vs 6 can mean 98% vs 2% weight.	transformer::attention transformer::softmax
What is the output of one attention operation for a single word?	A weighted sum of ALL Value vectors in the sequence, where weights come from softmax of (Q·K^T / √d_k). The result is a new vector that encodes the word's meaning <i>in context</i>. E.g., "sat" alone means "the verb sat" but after attention it means "sat, in the context of a cat on a mat."	transformer::attention
# --- Multi-Head Attention ---
Why use multiple attention heads instead of one?	Language has many simultaneous relationships: grammatical (subject-verb), spatial (location), syntactic (article-noun), etc. One set of Q/K/V weights can't capture all of these. Multiple heads (8 in the paper) each learn different relationship types. The paper's appendix shows one head learned verb dependencies and another learned pronoun resolution.	transformer::multihead
How are multi-head attention outputs combined?	Each head produces its own output vector. These are <b>concatenated</b> and then multiplied by a learned weight matrix W_O to project back to d_model dimensions:<br><br>MultiHead = Concat(head_1, ..., head_8) · W_O	transformer::multihead
How do multiple heads stay computationally efficient?	Instead of each head attending over all d_model=512 dimensions (8× compute), the dimensions are split: each head works on d_model/h = 512/8 = <b>64 dimensions</b>. Total compute ≈ same as single-head on 512 dims, but you get 8 different perspectives.	transformer::multihead
# --- Architecture ---
What are the two halves of the Transformer and what does each do?	<b>Encoder:</b> Reads the full input sequence and produces rich, context-aware representations for every input token. (6 identical layers)<br><b>Decoder:</b> Generates the output sequence one token at a time, attending both to its own previous outputs and to the encoder's output. (6 identical layers)	transformer::architecture
What sub-layers does each encoder layer have?	Two sub-layers:<br>1. <b>Multi-Head Self-Attention</b> — each word attends to all other input words<br>2. <b>Feed-Forward Network</b> (512 → 2048 → 512 with ReLU)<br><br>Each sub-layer has a <b>residual connection</b> and <b>layer normalization</b> around it: output = LayerNorm(x + Sublayer(x))	transformer::architecture transformer::encoder
What extra sub-layer does the decoder have compared to the encoder?	<b>Cross-attention</b> (between self-attention and FFN). Queries come from the decoder ("what am I generating?"), Keys and Values come from the encoder output ("what English words are available?"). This is how the decoder accesses the input. Every decoder layer receives the same encoder output.	transformer::architecture transformer::decoder transformer::cross-attention
In cross-attention, where do Q, K, and V come from?	<b>Q</b> comes from the <b>decoder</b> (previous decoder sub-layer output).<br><b>K and V</b> come from the <b>encoder's final output</b>.<br><br>The decoder is asking: "Given what I'm generating, which parts of the input should I pay attention to?"	transformer::architecture transformer::cross-attention
Why is masking needed in decoder self-attention?	During training, the decoder sees the entire output sentence at once. But at inference, it generates one word at a time. Masking prevents position i from attending to positions after i (seeing future tokens = cheating). Future positions get score = −∞ before softmax → weight = 0. It's a triangular mask. Training stays fully parallel despite this.	transformer::architecture transformer::decoder transformer::masking
What is a residual connection and why is it important?	output = x + Sublayer(x) — the original input is added back to the sub-layer's output. This gives gradients a "highway" to flow straight through, preventing vanishing gradients in deep networks. Without residuals, stacking 6+ layers would make training very difficult.	transformer::architecture transformer::residual
What is positional encoding and why is it needed?	Attention treats words as a set with no inherent order — "cat sat mat" and "mat sat cat" would produce identical scores. Positional encoding adds a unique position signal to each embedding before it enters the model:<br><br>input = word_embedding · √d_model + PE(position)<br><br>The paper uses sine/cosine waves at different frequencies (like clock hands at different speeds), giving each position a unique fingerprint. Values stay bounded [-1, 1] and may generalize to longer sequences.	transformer::architecture transformer::positional-encoding
What is the difference between heads and layers in the Transformer?	<b>Heads</b> (within one layer): Multiple perspectives on the same input — "8 people reading the same page with different glasses."<br><b>Layers</b> (stacked): Progressively deeper understanding — "reading the book 6 times, each read building on the previous." Layer 1 might learn basic relationships, layer 6 might learn high-level meaning by attending over already-enriched outputs.	transformer::architecture transformer::multihead
# --- Training ---
What loss function does the Transformer use for translation?	<b>Cross-entropy</b> over the full vocabulary (~37K tokens). At each position, the model predicts a probability distribution. Loss = −log(P(correct_word)). If the model assigns high probability to the correct word, loss is low. Summed across all output positions.	transformer::training transformer::loss
What is label smoothing and why does the paper use it?	Instead of training against hard targets [0, 0, 1, 0] (100% on correct word), use soft targets like [0.017, 0.017, 0.9, 0.017] — spreading ε=0.1 across all words. This prevents the model from becoming overconfident. It <i>hurts</i> perplexity (model is deliberately more uncertain) but <i>improves</i> actual translation quality (BLEU score).	transformer::training transformer::regularization
Why are token embeddings multiplied by √d_model before adding positional encodings?	Learned embeddings settle with small values (~variance 1/d_model), giving a vector magnitude of ~1. Positional encodings (sines/cosines) have magnitude ~√(d_model/2) ≈ 16. Without scaling, position would drown out word meaning. Multiplying by √d_model ≈ 22.6 boosts embeddings to comparable scale, so both word identity and position contribute meaningfully.	transformer::architecture transformer::positional-encoding transformer::training
Describe the Transformer's learning rate schedule.	Two phases:<br><b>Warmup</b> (first 4,000 steps): LR increases linearly. Early on, attention weights are random → gradients are noisy → high LR would cause wild updates.<br><b>Decay</b> (after 4,000 steps): LR decreases proportional to 1/√step. Gradually slows down for fine-tuning.<br><br>Uses Adam optimizer.	transformer::training transformer::optimizer
# --- Results ---
How did the base Transformer compare to previous SOTA on English→German?	Transformer base: <b>27.3 BLEU</b> vs previous best ensemble (ConvS2S): 26.36 BLEU. The base model beat every previous model <i>including ensembles</i> while costing <b>~20× less compute</b> (3.3×10^18 vs 7.7×10^19 FLOPs).	transformer::results
What happened when they varied the number of attention heads?	1 head: <b>0.9 BLEU drop</b> compared to 8 heads. 32 heads also dropped slightly. Sweet spot around 8-16 heads. This shows multiple heads are important but too many (with too-small dimensions per head) hurts quality.	transformer::results transformer::multihead
What three regularization techniques does the paper use?	1. <b>Dropout</b> (rate 0.1) — after each sub-layer and on embeddings. Removing it drops BLEU by 1.2 points.<br>2. <b>Label smoothing</b> (ε = 0.1) — softens training targets.<br>3. <b>Weight sharing</b> — input embedding, output embedding, and final linear layer share the same weight matrix. Major parameter saving.	transformer::training transformer::regularization
